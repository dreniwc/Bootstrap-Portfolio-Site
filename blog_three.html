Blog 3 - Website Changes

Improvments I have made to my site after feedback

Old Version Of Website

The feedback received from my tutors and peers along with comments from industry speakers has been very positive and motivational. Receiving a high mark has allowed me to believe in my skills and abilities within my university course and the web development industry.

The feedback from my tutors came during a ‘live marking session’ in which I was interviewed per say on elements they wanted to question, query and discuss. The first improvement was to change my approach to engaging my audience as a student but aim to portray myself to the audience more as a web developer, especially in my description along with avoiding ‘Welcome to…’.

The second point to improve on my site was within the back end code. I originally set the section headings to ‘H1’s’ but during the live marking discussion I found that although they are main headings for the sections they are required to be ‘H2’ for validation and accessibility.

Another comment made for my user usability was the header being consistent throughout the site, my tutor said “am I on the same page?”. I have adjusted the header for the separate pages as you can see below:

As shown above the blog pages now have a set height with a new different background image and formatting. I believe this works to a higher professional standard but remains consistent with my layout and design. I noticed from this change the benefit of having separate headers was a must for my usability.

A forth change I made to my site was to its responsive navigation. Originally the navigation was set with a breakpoint that allowed a typical portrait tablet to display the mobile, toggle menu. During the live marking my tutors said it would be more beneficial and user friendly to adjust the breakpoint for mobile only screens.

The images above show the changes. Viewing my portfolio site on a tablet is now clearer and more user friendly with the simple navigation.

The final change from my tutors was to frame my image. This was due to some having white space which merged into my site’s background giving an unclear perceptive on the image's true content. The examples below show how the images are now boxed with a simple border allowing for a cleaner, professional feel.

Mike Stephens, Digital Director of Ayup gave a talk during a lecture on 2nd December. With the opportunity for him to give opinions and comments on our portfolio sites, I was able to get feedback. He recommended applying some ‘design principles’ adding more spacing on the margins and padding that allow for a cleaner layout.

The improvements with more margins gives my site a more relaxed, spacious feel allowing for the content to be less bulked together in close proximities showing more usability and a user-friendly experience.

I found that all my feedback links to a quote from a Mell Ravenel Article “When designing a portfolio, you want a website that is straightforward. You want your content to be the focal point, rather than a distracting design” (Ravenel:online).

Overall I believe my first portfolio site was a success which met all required aspect. The minor tweaks have now been incorporated and show for an efficient and effective portfolio site.
